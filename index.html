Every 3D asset you see in a modern game, animated film or virtual production starts life as a high-poly sculpt — sometimes five, ten, even fifty million triangles. That data is beautiful, but it is unusable. You cannot rig it, you cannot animate it, and no real-time engine can render it. To bridge that gap, an artist must perform retopology by hand: redrawing the entire surface with a clean, low-polygon quad mesh. The numbers on screen are conservative — between eight and forty hours of expert work per asset, at studio rates of around one hundred and twenty dollars an hour. This bottleneck slows every production pipeline, drives up cost and burns out senior artists. So the question we kept asking ourselves was simple: can an AI agent take this job over, end to end, directly inside Blender?

Our answer is Neuralis. In one sentence: Neuralis is an autonomous agent that retopologises 3D meshes inside Blender, driven by natural-language intent. The workflow has three steps. Step one — input: the artist loads any high-poly mesh, whether it is a sculpt, a photogrammetry scan or a CAD export. Step two — the agentic loop: a large language model reads the mesh, plans a strategy, calls Blender's tools through the Model Context Protocol, observes the result, validates it, and iterates until the topology is clean. Step three — output: a quad-dominant, animation-ready low-poly mesh, fully shaded and ready to ship. The crucial shift is this: the artist no longer drives the mouse — they describe what they want, and the agent operates Blender directly. A five-million-triangle sculpt becomes a five-thousand-quad mesh, fully automatic. Now let's look at how it is built.

The engine is structured in five layers, from the metal up. Layer zero is the Blender Bridge — an MCP client that connects to Blender over TCP socket, stdio or Server-Sent Events. Layer one — Mesh Operations — wraps the seventy-one Blender tools we expose, plus checkpointing and bmesh templates. Layer two is the Math Engine, a separate Python subprocess that runs nine algorithms — Bayesian inference, Ransac, curvature analysis, feature-line detection, persistent homology, spatial queries and Thompson sampling — and talks back through an NDJSON pipe. Layer three is the orchestration brain. It runs an OODA-plus cycle — Observe, Orient, Decide, Act, Learn — coordinated by a blackboard, a directed acyclic graph of zones and a five-stage validator. The text model is Z.ai's GLM-4.7; the visual validator is GLM-4.6v. Layer four is the user-interface IPC layer. Every action is recorded in a PostgreSQL event store, learned rules go to Qdrant, and anti-patterns are kept in Neo4j. Every move the agent makes is logged — which brings us to our methods.

Let's talk about data and method. Neuralis is not a fine-tuned model — there is no static training set. Instead, we treat every retopology session as a data point. The inputs are real production meshes: artist sculpts, photogrammetry scans and CAD-derived primitives, ranging from a few thousand to several million triangles. The agent loop has two nested cycles. The outer cycle is OODA-plus: observe the mesh, orient against past experience, decide on a plan, act on Blender and learn from the outcome. The inner cycle is the ZoneWorker V2 loop: plan a small batch of edits, execute, compress the working memory using the language model itself, run anti-loop checks, validate, and submit a done-candidate to the coordinator. Quality is enforced by a five-stage validation cascade: hard topological constraints, soft quality metrics, visual checking by the vision model, cross-zone boundary checks, and a final regret gate. Every session is distilled into reusable rules and stored in Qdrant, so the system gets demonstrably better with every job it completes. Now — the numbers.
